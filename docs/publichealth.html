Training Resources

Websites for genomic epidemiology in public health

  • CDC’s Advanced Molecular Detection (AMD) page has a collection of resources and links out to additional resources such as the Pathogen Genomics Centers of Excellence. Another resource from AMD that I worked on with them is the CDC Genomic Epidemiology Toolkit. It’s a great resource and easy to work through since each module is self-contained and 10 minutes or less.

  • The Council of State and Territorial Epidemiologists (CSTE) has an Advanced Molecular Detection subcommittee that meets regularly and provides webinars. Interested CSTE members can join the meetings, and webinar recordings are available to all here.

  • The Association of Public Health Laboratories (APHL) provides training and resources through their Annual meeting as well as other special topics meetings, trainings and webinars that you can search through here.

  • State Public Health Bioinformatics Group (STAPH-B) is a consortium of bioinformaticians in public health working together to overcome barriers and share best practices. Recorded meetings and other trainings are linked through their website.

  • An applied genomic epidemiological handbook. by Alli Black and Gytis Dudas, available here.

  • Wellcome Connecting Science offers a variety of genomics and bioinformatics courses through FutureLearn

  • Food Safety Centers of Excellence offers a number of webinars and self-paced courses through their website

PMGR: Using Genomic Epidemiology to Advance Public Health Action In this grand rounds presentation, CAPT Ellie Click will describe how applications in genome science can be combined with epidemiology approaches to prevent, control, and respond to pathogens that threaten public health. Rapid advances in genomic technologies, such as genomic sequencing, are transforming how we respond to public health threats. Scientists use genomic sequencing to identify and prevent a wide variety of pathogens, including those that cause foodborne illness, tuberculosis, and COVID-19. Combining genomics and epidemiology—coined “genomic epidemiology”—provides a powerful tool for surveillance, outbreak detection, as well as response, and indicates a future where the human genome can direct public health action.
